## Claims
- Numerical weather prediction models can evaluate cloud seeding without relying solely on statistical field trials
- WRF model simulations show 5–15% precipitation enhancement under suitable orographic conditions
- Ground-based seeding generators are effective when meteorological conditions allow transport of seeding material to target clouds
- Model-based evaluation complements but does not replace randomized field experiments
- Seeding effectiveness is highly sensitive to wind direction, temperature, and cloud liquid water content

## Methods
WRF (Weather Research and Forecasting) model with explicit microphysics parameterization of silver iodide nucleation. Simulations of orographic cloud seeding over a western U.S. mountain range. Comparison of seeded vs. control model runs with identical initial conditions. Sensitivity analysis of meteorological parameters affecting seeding efficiency.

## Findings
5–15% precipitation enhancement in model simulations under optimal conditions. Seeding effectiveness drops sharply when temperature is above -5°C or below -25°C. Ground-based generators can deliver seeding material to cloud base altitudes under favourable upslope winds. Results consistent with physical theory and limited observational data.

## Limitations
- Model simulations cannot fully capture cloud microphysical complexity
- Results apply specifically to orographic winter cloud seeding
- Validation against observed seeding events is limited

## Relevance
Provides physical modeling evidence that complements statistical evaluations, helping to establish the mechanistic plausibility of precipitation enhancement from cloud seeding under specific conditions.

## Key Quotes
> "WRF simulations consistently produce 5–15% precipitation enhancement from silver iodide seeding of orographic winter clouds under meteorologically favorable conditions."
