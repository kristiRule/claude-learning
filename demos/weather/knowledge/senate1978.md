## Claims
- The U.S. government has funded weather modification research since Project Cirrus in 1947
- Operation Popeye (1967–1972) was a classified military program that used cloud seeding over Vietnam to extend the monsoon season and disrupt enemy supply lines
- The U.S. conducted cloud seeding experiments over Cuba, the Philippines, and domestic states
- Congress found that weather modification posed potential risks requiring federal regulation
- No comprehensive national policy existed to govern civilian or military weather modification programs

## Methods
Congressional investigation drawing on testimony from NOAA, Department of Defense, and independent scientists. Review of classified and declassified military weather modification records. Survey of 28 federally-funded and state-funded cloud seeding programs operating in 1978.

## Findings
Operation Popeye successfully extended monsoon rainfall in targeted areas of Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia, demonstrating military utility of weather modification. Civilian programs in 28 states targeted hail suppression and precipitation enhancement. The report found fragmented federal oversight across multiple agencies with no coordinating authority. International concern existed about U.S. weather modification activities affecting neighboring countries.

## Limitations
- Military program data remains partially classified
- Independent verification of Operation Popeye efficacy claims is limited by classification
- Historical report; regulatory landscape has changed significantly since 1978

## Relevance
The foundational government document establishing that the U.S. conducted extensive weather modification programs, including classified military operations. Provides historical context for understanding the scope and government acceptance of intentional atmospheric intervention.

## Key Quotes
> "Operation Popeye demonstrated that cloud seeding technology could be weaponized to affect weather patterns over hostile territory, raising profound ethical and legal questions."

> "The United States has no comprehensive national policy to govern weather modification activities, leaving a regulatory vacuum that could allow harmful or irresponsible programs to proceed unchecked."

> "Weather modification is no longer a speculative technology — it is an operational reality practiced by federal agencies, state governments, and private operators."
