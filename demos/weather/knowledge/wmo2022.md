## Claims
- 56 countries operate active weather modification programs as of 2022
- Cloud seeding is the most widely practiced form of intentional weather modification globally
- Evidence for precipitation enhancement ranges from 5% to 30% depending on cloud type and climate
- WMO calls for mandatory randomized controlled trial designs in national programs
- Transboundary weather modification requires international coordination and notification protocols

## Methods
Global survey of national weather modification programs across WMO member states. Synthesis of peer-reviewed literature on cloud seeding efficacy 1950–2022. Expert panel review by leading atmospheric scientists from member nations. Policy analysis of national regulations and international agreements.

## Findings
Precipitation enhancement of 5–30% is achievable under suitable meteorological conditions. Orographic cloud seeding shows the most consistent positive results. Convective cloud seeding results are more variable. No documented cases of catastrophic unintended consequences, but localized effects on neighboring regions are poorly understood. Most programs lack rigorous evaluation frameworks.

## Limitations
- Survey relies on voluntary self-reporting from member states; some programs may be undisclosed
- Efficacy data quality varies dramatically across programs
- Long-term cumulative effects of large-scale operational programs not assessed

## Relevance
As the authoritative global body on atmospheric science, WMO's review represents the scientific consensus position on weather modification. Its call for standardized evaluation and international coordination is the most credible policy recommendation available.

## Key Quotes
> "Weather modification, particularly cloud seeding, is practiced in over 50 countries, yet rigorous scientific evaluation of efficacy remains the exception rather than the rule."

> "WMO recommends that all national weather modification programs adopt randomized experimental designs and report outcomes through internationally standardized protocols."

> "The potential for weather modification activities in one nation to affect precipitation patterns in neighboring countries represents an emerging governance challenge."
