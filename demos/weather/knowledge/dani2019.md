## Claims
- Karnataka's Varshadhare program targeted drought-affected agricultural regions
- Hygroscopic flare seeding from aircraft produced a 24% increase in daily rainfall in target areas
- The program demonstrates that developing-country governments can operate effective seeding programs
- Suitable cloud targets were identified by radar and satellite before each seeding mission
- Results are consistent with other hygroscopic seeding studies in tropical regions

## Methods
Aircraft-based hygroscopic flare seeding targeting convective clouds identified by radar. Statistical comparison of rainfall on seeded days vs. historical climatology and nearby unseeded reference areas. Multiple flight seasons over water-stressed agricultural districts.

## Findings
24% increase in daily rainfall reported in target areas on seeded days compared to control. Radar-measured cloud evolution shows accelerated precipitation development after seeding. The program was judged cost-effective relative to alternative water supply infrastructure.

## Limitations
- Absence of true randomized control group
- Rainfall measurement network density in rural Karnataka limits spatial precision
- 24% enhancement figure may reflect selection bias toward days with already-favorable cloud conditions

## Relevance
Demonstrates government-funded cloud seeding as drought mitigation in an agricultural developing-country context. The Karnataka program influenced similar programs in other Indian states.

## Key Quotes
> "Analysis of rainfall data from the Varshadhare program indicates a 24% increase in daily rainfall in target areas, providing operational justification for continued program investment."
