## Claims
- Stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI) could reduce global mean temperatures by 1–2°C
- SAI would disrupt regional precipitation patterns, with potential for drought in some areas
- The termination shock — abrupt warming if SAI is suddenly stopped — is a major governance risk
- No international body has authority to govern or prohibit SAI deployment
- SAI is distinct from cloud seeding and operates at a planetary scale with global effects

## Methods
Review of climate model simulations from CMIP6 and GeoMIP model intercomparison projects. Analysis of governance frameworks and international law applicable to geoengineering. Literature review of proposed delivery mechanisms and cost estimates.

## Findings
SAI using sulfur dioxide injection could offset 1–2°C of warming globally at costs of $2–8 billion per year — far cheaper than mitigation. Side effects include reduced global precipitation by 1–3%, ozone layer thinning, and altered monsoon patterns. No nation or international body has authority to prevent a single actor from deploying SAI unilaterally.

## Limitations
- Climate model projections of SAI effects carry large uncertainties, especially for regional precipitation
- Real-world deployment effects cannot be tested without deployment
- Governance analysis reflects 2022 treaty landscape

## Relevance
SAI is the form of weather modification with the most dramatic global implications. Unlike cloud seeding, which is local, SAI would affect precipitation globally — making it the most consequential and contested form of atmospheric intervention.

## Key Quotes
> "Stratospheric aerosol injection could be deployed by a single nation or even a wealthy non-state actor, yet its effects would be felt globally — making it fundamentally a global governance challenge."

> "The termination shock risk means that once SAI is initiated at scale, stopping it abruptly could cause warming faster than if it had never been deployed."
