## Claims
- North Dakota has operated one of the longest-running cloud seeding programs in the U.S.
- The primary objectives are hail suppression and precipitation enhancement for agriculture
- Crop yield data shows modest positive correlations with program activity in hail damage reduction
- The economic case for cloud seeding is stronger for hail suppression than for rainfall augmentation
- Long-term program continuity enables trend analysis not possible in short-duration experiments

## Methods
Time series analysis of crop yield records, hail damage insurance claims, and precipitation data over multiple decades of the North Dakota Cloud Modification Project. Comparison of seeded counties vs. neighboring unseeded counties using difference-in-differences approach.

## Findings
Statistically significant reduction in insured hail damage losses in counties with active seeding. Rainfall augmentation effects are less clearly attributable to seeding given natural variability. Benefit-cost ratios for hail suppression exceed 10:1 in some years, providing economic justification for program continuation.

## Limitations
- Non-randomized observational design; cannot rule out confounding factors
- Adjacent counties may benefit from spillover seeding effects, reducing apparent contrast
- Agricultural benefit calculations sensitive to commodity price assumptions

## Relevance
Provides a long-running U.S. domestic case study of weather modification in an agricultural context, demonstrating that hail suppression is among the most economically justified applications of cloud seeding technology.

## Key Quotes
> "Hail suppression represents the most economically defensible application of cloud seeding, with benefit-cost ratios consistently exceeding those claimed for precipitation enhancement."
