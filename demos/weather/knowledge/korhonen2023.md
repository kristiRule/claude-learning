## Claims
- Climate change is driving increased government investment in weather modification as a water security response
- There is a significant gap between the scale of operational cloud seeding programs and the peer-reviewed evidence base
- No universally accepted international regulatory framework governs transboundary weather modification
- Standardized evaluation protocols are urgently needed to assess program efficacy
- Water-scarce nations are deploying cloud seeding operationally before scientific consensus is established

## Methods
Systematic review of peer-reviewed literature on cloud seeding efficacy. Survey of national weather modification policies across 30+ countries. Analysis of funding trends for weather modification research 2000–2022. Policy analysis of international agreements touching on transboundary atmospheric modification.

## Findings
Over 50 countries operate weather modification programs, but fewer than 10% use randomized experimental designs to evaluate efficacy. Claimed precipitation increases range from 5% to 30% but methodological inconsistencies make comparisons unreliable. Climate change is accelerating program deployment faster than scientific evaluation can validate outcomes.

## Limitations
- Policy review may not capture unpublished or classified national programs
- Efficacy data relies heavily on self-reporting by program operators
- Transboundary effects are largely unquantified

## Relevance
Provides the most current policy-level overview of global weather modification as a climate adaptation strategy, highlighting the governance gap between operational scale and scientific validation.

## Key Quotes
> "The rapid expansion of operational weather modification programs in the context of climate-driven water scarcity is outpacing our ability to rigorously evaluate their effectiveness."

> "No internationally binding framework currently governs weather modification that crosses national boundaries, creating potential for conflict as programs expand."
